### Why this post?

My dive into the world of personal computing ended up changing the direction of my career. The Apple ][+ was the first step along that path. My interests moved away from the mainframes at this point, never to seriously return. Yes, I would continue to learn and work in a mainframe environment, but my personal efforts were dedicated to the desktop.

#### TL;DR

A computer on my desk! Ooh, shiny!

### What to buy?

After really settling in to my job, I started getting the "itch" again to work on personal projects. But even though there were many more choices in 1981, they were all still very expensive. A fully equipped system was in the neighborhood of $2000 - not much different than 1979. The difference was that in 1981 the systems were much more powerful. However, that $2000 was roughly 4 months pay for an E-3 with two years service and therefore a very sizable investment.

#### First encounter

Being very risk-adverse, I started by finding other personal computing enthusiasts on base. The first was someone who owned a fairly complete S-100 bus system with an 8080 (might have been a Z-80) CPU, terminal, printer, and modem. He showed me the basics of CP/M, including what needed to be done to configure, assemble, and install a BIOS to work with his hardware configuration. Writing the BIOS for his system then technically became my first real program for a personal computer.

He then showed me just how much he had invested in it for both the hardware and software - that quickly put it well out of reach.

#### Smart advice

I had a number of very detailed conversations with our lead Honeywell CE. He kept telling me that I had to seriously consider the "new computer from IBM" that was going to be released soon. His opinion was that "IBM was going to completely change the world of personal computing." No, I didn't believe him. I remember telling him that I didn't see how IBM's sales channel would be able to handle the volume of sales to the general public, and that the major vendors at the time were already well entrenched in businesses.

This was only the first of many industry-related predictions that I was going to get completely and hilariously wrong.

#### Second encounter

I met another individual who owned a fairly decked-out Apple ][. In his computer he had a serial card with two ports, the language card (a 16K memory expansion), the Z-80 card, and a floppy disk controller card. He also had a terminal connected to the serial card.

The terminal was text only - a tv was still needed to show any graphics. But if I remember correctly, he could show the graphics on the TV while still working on his program in the terminal.


### Apple ][+ for the win

I decided on the Apple ][+ with 48K, with the disk controller and one disk drive, and the parallel port interface. The Apple came with a pair of very detailed manuals, describing the hardware and software.
